9. If 6.60 moles h2 are made to react with 4.42 moles n2, how many moles nh3 will be produced? *


SOLUTION:

Step 1: Write the balanced chemical equation.

N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of NH₃ formed by each reactant.

• Using N₂

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 1 mole of N₂ is stoichiometrically equivalent to 2 moles of NH₃.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{moles of} \: \text{NH}_3 & = \text{4.42 mol} \: \text{N}_2 \times \frac{\text{2 mol} \: \text{NH}_3}{\text{1 mol} \: \text{N}_2} \\ & = \text{8.84 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

• Using H₂

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 3 moles of H₂ is stoichiometrically equivalent to 2 moles of NH₃.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{moles of} \: \text{NH}_3 & = \text{6.60 mol} \: \text{H}_2 \times \frac{\text{2 mol} \: \text{NH}_3}{\text{3 mol} \: \text{H}_2} \\ & = \text{4.40 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Determine the limiting reagent.

Since H₂ produced less amount of NH₃ than N₂, H₂ is the limiting reagent.

Step 4: Determine the number of moles of NH₃ formed.

Note that the number of moles of a product formed is dictated by the limiting reagent.

[tex]\text{moles of} \: \text{NH}_3 = \boxed{\text{4.40 mol}}[/tex]

Hence, the number of moles of NH₃ that will be produced is 4.40 mol.

A 125 mole of this fuel undergone complete combustion with the aid of 2900 mole dry air. Determine the percent excess oxygen, the orsat analysis of the fuel gas, and the complete analysis of the flue gas. The reaction for the combustion of H2S is: H2S + O2 → H2O + SO2​


Answer:

To solve this problem, we need to first write the balanced equation for the combustion of the fuel:

C3H8 + 5O2 → 3CO2 + 4H2O

C4H10 + 6.5O2 → 4CO2 + 5H2O

CO + 0.5O2 → CO2

H2 + 0.5O2 → H2O

H2S + 1.5O2 → H2O + SO2

Note that we need 5 moles of oxygen for every mole of C3H8, and 6.5 moles of oxygen for every mole of C4H10.

Now we can determine the stoichiometric air required for the combustion of 125 moles of fuel:

7.5/2 + 71.25/8 + 3/2 + 6.25/2 + 18.75/10 + 10/4 + x = 125

where x is the moles of oxygen in the stoichiometric air. Solving for x, we get:

x = 592.5 moles

Therefore, the percent excess oxygen is:

(2900 - 592.5)/592.5 * 100% = 390.9%

Next, we can determine the Orsat analysis of the fuel gas. The Orsat analysis measures the concentrations of CO2, O2, and CO in the fuel gas after it has been passed through an absorption apparatus. We can calculate the expected concentrations of these gases based on the stoichiometry of the combustion reaction:

CO2: (371.25/8 + 418.75/10)/125 = 0.398

O2: (2900 - 592.5 - 125*x)/125 = 0.118

CO: (10/4)/125 = 0.008

Therefore, the Orsat analysis of the fuel gas is 0.398% CO2, 0.118% O2, and 0.008% CO.

Finally, we can determine the complete analysis of the flue gas, which measures the concentrations of CO2, O2, CO, H2O, and SO2 in the flue gas. We can calculate the expected concentrations of these gases based on the stoichiometry of the combustion reaction:

CO2: (371.25/8 + 418.75/10)/125 * 100% = 31.8%

O2: (2900 - 592.5 - 125x)/2900 * 100% = 2.1%

CO: (10/4)/125 * 100% = 0.64%

H2O: ((371.25/8 + 418.75/10) + 4125)/125 * 100% = 21.5%

SO2: (7.5/2)/125 * 100% = 3.0%

Therefore, the complete analysis of the flue gas is 31.8% CO2, 2.1% O2, 0.64% CO, 21.5% H2O, and 3.0% SO2.

29. how many moles of H2O are produced by the conplete reaction of 10.0 mole of H2 and 5.0 mole of 02?​


Solution:

Step 1: Write the balanced equation.

2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O

Step 2: Determine the mole ratios needed.

Using H₂

mole ratio = 2 mol H₂ : 2 mol H₂O

Using O₂

mole ratio = 1 mol O₂ : 2 mol H₂O

Step 3: Calculate the number of moles of H₂O produced by each reactant.

Using H₂

[tex]\text{moles of H₂O = 10.0 mol H₂} × \frac{\text{2 mol H₂O}}{\text{2 mol H₂}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of H₂O = 10.0 mol}[/tex]

Using O₂

[tex]\text{moles of H₂O = 5.0 mol O₂} × \frac{\text{2 mol H₂O}}{\text{1 mol O₂}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of H₂O = 10.0 mol}[/tex]

Step 4: Determine the limiting reactant.

Since the two reactants produce equal number of moles of H₂O

[tex]\text{There is no limiting reactant. The reactants are both consumed.}[/tex]

Step 5: Determine the number of moles of H₂O produced.

Since there is no limiting reactant

[tex]\boxed{\text{moles of H₂O = 10.0 mol}}[/tex]
