The metric system, also known as the International System of Units (SI), is a system of measurement that is based on the meter, kilogram, and second as its base units. The system is widely used around the world and is the standard system of measurement in most countries.

The key feature of the metric system is its use of prefixes that indicate a multiple or fraction of the base units. For example, the prefix "kilo-" means 1,000 times the base unit, so a kilometer is 1,000 meters. Other prefixes include "milli-" (1/1,000), "micro-" (1/1,000,000), "nano-" (1/1,000,000,000), and so on.

Another important feature of the metric system is its decimal nature, which makes it easy to understand and use. For example, a meter is divided into 100 centimeters and a liter is divided into 1,000 milliliters.

The metric system is used to measure a wide range of quantities including length, mass, volume, temperature, and time. The system is designed to be coherent, which means that the units of different quantities are related to each other in a logical way. For example, the units of length, area and volume all are coherent, where cm^2 (square centimeter) is derived from cm (centimeter) and m^3 (cubic meter) is derived from m (meter)

The International System of Units (SI) is based on the International System of Units that is widely accepted as the standard system of measurement for scientific, technical and most other international use, today.
