TexturesMonophonic texturePolyphonic textureMonophonic textureMonophonic texturePolyphonic textureMonophonic texture

Monophony occurs when a complete melody is played by two or more instruments or sung by a choir with a fixed interval, such as a perfect fifth (or "monophonic"). Many traditional and folk tunes are monophonic. If a group of singers sings the same melody in unison (at the same pitch) or with the same melody notes reproduced at the octave, the song is considered monophonic (such as when men and women sing together). Monophony is the simplest of musical textures, consisting of a melody (or "song") sung or played by a single singer or instrument player without accompanying harmony or chords.

Polyphonic texture

Polyphony is a term used in the Western musical heritage to describe music from the late Middle Ages and Renaissance periods. Although both homophony and polyphony express emotion, polyphony is better at expressing numerous emotions in a single piece of music due to the multiple melodic lines. As the melodies weave over one other, the extra lines lend texture to the melody. Polyphony is a sort of musical texture that consists of two or more simultaneous lines of independent melody, as opposed to monophony, which has only one voice, and homophony, which has only one dominant melodic voice accompanied by chords.

Homophonic texture

A homophonic texture is one in which numerous notes are played at the same time but all have the same melody. The homophonic texture is when a rock or pop star sings a song while playing guitar or keyboard at the same time. Melody-dominated homophony is the most common style of homophony, in which one vocal, usually the highest, plays a distinct tune while the other voices collaborate to articulate an underlying harmony. A homophonic texture might be homorhythmic, meaning that all of the sections have the same beat. Another homophony type is chorale texture.
