14. What is the literal translation of the term "Bao"? Why Do you think is the dance called "Bao dance"?​


Answer:

What is Bao Dance?

One of the traditional dances in the Philippines is Bao Dance. This is also known as Magbabao where "bao" means a coconut shell.

This dance is called as that because dancers use coconut shells as props. Coconut shells are cut in halves and they are secured onto the dancers' hands, knees, elbows and back. It is performed by hitting one coconut shell with the others. Dancers sometimes hit the ones on the hands, sometimes the ones on the body, and sometimes the shells worn by another performer, all in time to a fast drumbeat.

Explanation:

How did it originate?

It is danced to mimic the early battle against Christians and Muslim Moros during the time of Spanish colonization.

How it is performed?

This is performed by all-male dancers who wear blue pants to represent the Christians and red pant for the Muslim Moros. All dancers have coconut shells mounted on their body.

This dance form is also known as "Maglalatik" dance as the prize for winning the war was latik. Latik is the residue obtained after boiling the coconut milk.

Explanation:

Maglalatik is a folk dance that originated from the Philippines. This dance includes four or six coconut shell halves also known as bao. Maglalatik Dance

The Maglalatik is a folk dance from the Philippines performed by male dancers. Coconut shell halves are secured onto the dancers' hands and on vests upon which are hung four or six more coconut shell halves.

It originated in the town of Binan in the Laguna province of the Filipino Islands and is also performed as homage to the town saint, San Isidro de Labrador. How is it performed? Maglalatik is an original native dance of the Philippines in which the coconut halves are attached to the torso of the dancer.

Findings revealed that the prominent dance steps in the Philippine local dance Maglalatik are the (1) jogging, (2) forward-backward step, (3) arm swing, and (4) 8 quick clap cycles.

This dance is composed of a four-part performance and the first two performance of the dance is called as the 'Palipasan' and the 'Baligtaran'. In these first two performances, the dance will show the opposing squads in an intense battle. The last two parts of the dance are the 'Paseo' and the 'Sayaw Escaramusa'.

Maglalatik is a positive song by EDDIE MUNJI III with a tempo of 116 BPM. It can also be used half-time at 58 BPM or double-time at 232 BPM.
