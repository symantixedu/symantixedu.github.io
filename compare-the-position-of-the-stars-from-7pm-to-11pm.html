The Position of Stars in the Sky Changes

The position of stars in the sky changes because of the rotation of the Earth. Just like how the sun changes position in the sky, the moon and stars are the same. Every day, the Earth rotates which means when you are looking at a specific point in the sky without moving, you would see that the sky changes from day to night. This is also true with the stars.

However, the position of stars is also relative to their location or the viewers' location. There are stars that looked like stationary such as the North Star, also known as Polaris, and those that are near to it. This is because they are closely above the North and South Pole wherein these poles remain stationary while the Earth rotates from west to east. The stars may only become visible or invisible.

More Insights Why the Position of the Stars in the Sky Changes

Aside from the Earth's rotation, the position of stars in the sky changes because constellations are constantly moving. Nevertheless, their movements are not measured by hours nor days. Rather, it might take thousands of years for them to move.

Focus on one or two constellation like the Auriga and Orion which are the best seen in the month of December. 3. Look at the stars clearly every hour of the night from 7pm to 11pm. Guide Question: Q5. Compare the position of the Stars in the sky. What do you notice? Q6. Are you the stars visible at 7pm still visible at 11pm in their "Original position?" Q7. How does stars move? Describe the movement of the Stars in the night sky Q8. How is the motion of the Stars similar to the motion of the Sun?​


Answer:

When observing the stars in the night sky from 7pm to 11pm, it is noticeable that their position changes. One or two constellations, such as Auriga and Orion, can be focused on to better observe this movement. Comparing the position of the stars at 7pm to their position at 11pm, it can be seen that they are no longer in their original position, having moved across the sky. The stars appear to move in a circular motion around the North Star, with some stars even disappearing from view as they dip below the horizon. This movement is similar to the motion of the sun, which rises in the east and sets in the west due to the Earth's rotation on its axis. Therefore, the apparent movement of the stars through the night sky is a result of the Earth's rotation on its axis.
