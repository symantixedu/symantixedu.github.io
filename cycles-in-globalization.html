10. How does global warming affect the life cycle of living things???


Answer:

Climate change also alters the life cycles of plants and animals. For example, as temperatures get warmer, many plants are starting to grow and bloom earlier in the spring and survive longer into the fall. Some animals are waking from hibernation sooner or migrating at different times, too.

Answer:

Climate change also alters the life cycles of plants and animals. For example, as temperatures get warmer, many plants are starting to grow and bloom earlier in the spring and survive longer into the fall. Some animals are waking from hibernation sooner or migrating at different times, too.

How does global warming affect animals?

The key impact of global warming on wildlife is habitat disruption, in which ecosystems—places where animals have spent millions of years adapting—rapidly transform in response to climate change, reducing their ability to fulfill the species' needs.

How will climate change affect us?

Health. Human health is vulnerable to climate change. The changing environment is expected to cause more heat stress, an increase in waterborne diseases, poor air quality, and diseases transmitted by insects and rodents. Extreme weather events can compound many of these health threats.

How does global warming affect living things?

Humans and wild animals face new challenges for survival because of climate change. More frequent and intense drought, storms, heat waves, rising sea levels, melting glaciers and warming oceans can directly harm animals, destroy the places they live, and wreak havoc on people's livelihoods and communities.

Answer:

1. Over the past few hundred thousand years, the level of CO2 in the atmosphere has been closely correlated with the global temperature. During times when the level of CO2 in the atmosphere was high, the global temperature was generally higher, and during times when the level of CO2 was low, the global temperature was generally lower. This relationship is supported by evidence from various sources, including ice cores, which show that the level of CO2 in the atmosphere has varied over time and has been closely linked to changes in the global temperature.

2. Feedback loops are processes in which a change in one part of a system leads to a change in another part of the system, which in turn leads to further changes in the first part of the system. In the context of climate and climate systems, feedback loops can either amplify or dampen the initial change. For example, one common feedback loop in the climate system is the water vapor feedback, which occurs when rising temperatures lead to an increase in the amount of water vapor in the atmosphere, which in turn leads to further warming due to the greenhouse effect.

3. The hydrologic cycle is the process by which water moves through the Earth's surface, atmosphere, and oceans. Water evaporates from the surface of the Earth and rises into the atmosphere, where it forms clouds and eventually falls back to the surface as precipitation. This water then flows into rivers, lakes, and oceans, where it can be used by plants, animals, and humans. Water management refers to the ways in which water is collected, stored, and distributed for various uses. If the climate were to warm up significantly, the hydrologic cycle could be affected in various ways, including increased evaporation and changes in the amount and distribution of precipitation. Similarly, if the climate were to cool down significantly, the hydrologic cycle could be affected by changes in the amount and distribution of precipitation and the amount of water available for use.

4. Both natural and human-induced processes have influenced global climate change. Natural processes include changes in solar radiation, volcanic eruptions, and changes in the Earth's orbit, which can affect the amount of energy that is received from the sun and the distribution of that energy around the Earth. Human-induced processes include the burning of fossil fuels, which releases greenhouse gases into the atmosphere, and land use changes, which can alter the Earth's ability to absorb or reflect energy from the sun. These processes have contributed to changes in the Earth's climate both in the past and present. The greatest factor influencing global climate change is currently the release of greenhouse gases into the atmosphere, particularly carbon dioxide, which is released through the burning of fossil fuels and other activities.
