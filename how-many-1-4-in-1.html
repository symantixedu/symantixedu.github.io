DIVIDING FRACTIONS

A rule when dividing fractions:

Before you divide the fractions, find the reciprocal of the divisor then turn the operation into a multiplication.

[tex]4 \div \frac{1}{6} = n \\ \frac{4}{1} \div \frac{1}{6} = n \\ \frac{4}{1} \times \frac{6}{1} = \frac{24}{1} = 24[/tex]

Answer:

a) No. However 4 is a member of {4}. (Indeed, it’s t he only member.)

The brackets denote a very different mathematical being ( a set) from the mathematical beings known as integers

b) A set is determined completely by its members. This is the Axiom of Extension (or Extensionality).

Therefore as sets, {3,4,3,5} is exactly the same set as {3,4,5} . So there are 3 elements in the set.

Note that the set can also be described as {4,3,3,3,4,5} . Neither order nor repetition change the identity of the set.

DIVIDING FRACTIONS

How many 1/8s are there in 3/4?

Divide 3/4 by 1/8.

[tex] \frac{3}{4} \div \frac{1}{8} = \frac{3}{4} \div \frac{8}{1} = \frac{24}{4} \div 4 = \boxed {6}[/tex]

There are 6 of 1/8 in 3/4.

How many 1/6s are there in 2/3?

Same process.

[tex] \frac{2}{3} \div \frac{1}{6} = \frac{2}{3} \div \frac{6}{1} = \frac{12}{3} \div 3 = \boxed {4}[/tex]

There are 4 of 1/6 in 2/3.

How many 1 1/4 are there in 8 1/2?

Same process but, convert first the mixed number into improper fraction.

[tex]1 \frac{1}{4} = \frac{5}{4} \: \: \bold {and \: \: } 8 \frac{1}{2} = \frac{17}{2} [/tex]

Now, divide.

[tex] \frac{17}{2} \div \frac{5}{4} = \frac{17}{2} \div \frac{4}{5} = \frac{68}{10} \div 2 = \boxed { \frac{34}{5} \: \: \bold {or} \: \: 6 \frac{4}{5} } [/tex]

There are 6 4/5 of 1 1/4 in 8 1/2.

How many 1 3/4 are there in 12 1/2?

Same process as the third one.

[tex]1 \frac{3}{4} = \frac{7}{4} \: \: \bold {and} \: \: 12 \frac{1}{2} = \frac{25}{2} \\ \\ \frac{25}{2} \div \frac{7}{4} = \frac{25}{2} \div \frac{4}{7} = \frac{100}{14} \\ \\ \frac{100}{14} \div 2 = \boxed { \frac{50}{7} \: \: \bold {or } \: \: 7\frac{1}{7} } [/tex]

There are 7 1/7 of 1 3/4 in 12 1/2.
