24. "Heard melodies are sweet but thoseunheandare sweeter"​


Answer:

The above quote “Heard melodies are sweet, but those unheard are sweeter” appears in the second stanza of the poem “Ode on a Grecian Urn” by John Keats published in the year 1820.

Explanation:

The above lines are the first instance in the poem where the idea of superiority of art over ordinary life is propagated. “Heard melodies” represent the reality or real life or our daily mundane life, while “those unheard” represent the life of art or imagination.

Everything in life has its end, so has music, it is transient in nature. In our daily lives, we listen to songs or music when we want to or for just a moment. We feel a momentary pleasure and bliss. But the music which the “Grecian Urn” presents through the musician in the poem is even sweeter than the heard melodies of the real or ordinary world. As the musician playing the flute or music is captured in the “Grecian Urn”, it is completely frozen in time and no one can stop the mellifluous music, the music will be continually played in the eternity(foster child of time, refer to 1st stanza). Even though we cannot hear the music from the Urn John Keats forces us to perceive it through our imagination, which is more powerful than the physical senses. Thus the music would never come to an end and we can enjoy it for an indefinite period of time.

John Keats allows the readers to sense the things by themselves with their ability in order to have a complete understanding and enriching experience, rather than making everything cooked for readers. This technique of sensuality is used by Keats throughout the poem.

The above lines can also be considered paradoxical or self-contradictory as the “unheard” melodies can never be heard in physical senses or it never exists in the real world, as it can never be heard, it is still sweeter only if we perceive it through our imaginary senses which is far more superior than our physical senses.
