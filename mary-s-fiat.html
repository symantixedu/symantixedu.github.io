14. Analyze how the transition from the Gold Standard to Fiat Money changed global finance and trade, and discuss how Fiat Money has evolved to impact the contemporary world.​


The transition from the Gold Standard to Fiat Money was a significant shift in global finance and trade, with significant consequences for the world economy. Prior to this transition, many countries adhered to the Gold Standard, which meant that their currency was based on a fixed gold par. In other words, their currency was tied to a certain amount of gold, which provided a certain amount of stability to their currency.

However, during the early 20th century, the Gold Standard began to falter, especially with the onset of World War I. Countries found it increasingly difficult to maintain their currency’s link to gold, and eventually abandoned it altogether. This led to the rise of Fiat Money, which is unbacked by a fixed asset and is subject to inflation and deflation based on market forces.

The transition from the Gold Standard to Fiat Money has had profound effects on global finance and trade. Fiat Money has allowed countries to print as much money as they need, which can lead to inflation and currency devaluation, but also provides flexibility in times of economic stress. Additionally, the rise of Fiat Money has led to the increased use of credit and debt, both of which have had significant impacts on world economic systems.

Modern Fiat Money has evolved to become more sophisticated, with central banks playing a critical role in maintaining the stability of national currencies. This has led to financial systems dominated by large banks and financial institutions, as well as the increased use of electronic currency systems.

Overall, the transition from the Gold Standard to Fiat Money has had a profound effect on global finance and trade. It has led to the creation of modern financial systems, which have both benefits and drawbacks. The evolution of Fiat Money has changed the nature of the world economy, and will continue to shape it in the years to come.

27. discuss fiat currency and its effectsmm discuss their introduction ​


Answer:

Fiat money is a currency (a medium of exchange) established as money, often by government regulation, but that does not have intrinsic value (value independent of the nominal value, such as a precious metal might have). Fiat money does not have use value (inherent utility, such as a cow or beaver pelt might have), and has value only because a government maintains its value, or because parties engaging in exchange agree on its value.[1] It was introduced as an alternative to commodity money (a medium which has its own intrinsic value) and representative money (money which represents something with intrinsic value). Representative money is similar to fiat money, but it represents a claim on a commodity (which can be redeemed to a greater or lesser extent).[2][3][a]

Government-issued banknotes were used first during the 11th century in China. Since then, they have been used by various countries, usually concurrently with commodity currencies. Fiat money started to predominate during the 20th century. Since President Nixon's decision to decouple the US dollar from gold in 1971, a system of national fiat currencies has been used globally.

30. Differentiate fiat money from commodity money?


Answer:

Fiat Money- A form of money which is a legal tender but doesn’t have any intrinsic value.

For example : Paper Currency. Without the numerical Value written over it, it’s a mere piece of paper. It cannot be traded for anything.

Commodity Money- A form of money which is a legal tender and has some intrinsic value.

For example : Gold, silver.

Also, Commodity money is somewhat universally recognized and accepted. You can trade in gold or silver every market in the world. However, Fiat money can be used only in specific markets( e.g. Dollar cannot be used to transact in Indian market). To use them in other markets, they have to be exchanged with the regional fiat currency.
