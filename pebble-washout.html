Pebbles are defined as particles between 4mm and 64mm in diameter and cobbles up to 256mm.xplanation:

Sung Kwun Chough, in Geology and Sedimentology of the Korean Peninsula, 2013

4.1.2.3 Pebble-Bearing Phyllites

The pebble-bearing phyllites of the Hwanggangri Formation occur in an extensive area (Figure 4.1). Clasts constitute 3–30% of the rock by volume. Quartzite clasts are predominant, which are well rounded (Figure 4.2). Some clasts of quartzite are large (more than 2 m in diameter). Others comprise pebble-size quartzite, elongated shale, limestone, and dolomite (Figure 4.3) (Chough and Bahk, 1992). Shale clasts are of the same composition as the matrix, displaying a wide range of deformed shape (Figure 4.3). Limestone clasts are also abundant, including chips, pebbles, and boulders, and show various deformational structures such as flattening, foliation, and folding. Other clasts are minor, including granite, sandstone, and metamorphic rock fragments. Matrices are either siliciclastic or calcareous (calciclastic) and poorly sorted. They range from 70% to 97% by volume. Assemblages of these clasts differ from region to region (Chough and Bahk, 1992).

Figure 4.3. (A) Line drawing of pebble-bearing phyllite (Hwanggangri Formation) showing various clasts of quartzite, limestone, sandstone, and black shale in the Suanbo area. (B) A schematic diagram showing slope failure and progressive development of debris flow.

Source: Chough and Bahk (1992).

The pebble-bearing phyllites are interpreted as tillites (Reedman and Um, 1975; Reedman et al., 1973) or glaciogenic deposits of snowball Earth (Choi et al., 2012). Although the occurrence of isolated dropstones suggests melting of icebergs, the associated sedimentary facies successions are not indicative of extensive glaciation. The existence of contemporaneous limestone and shale chips suggests that these deposits formed by resedimentation during slump/slide and subsequent debris flows in slope and base-of-slope environments (Chough, 1981; Chough and Bahk, 1992). Quartzites and granites originated from local denudation, most likely during the period of sea-level fall (Figure 4.4). The clasts of shale and limestone chips originated from slope failure of the contemporaneous substrates. The breakage of contemporaneous mud commonly occurs in slope and base-of-slope environments (Chough and Bahk, 1984–1985; Yoon et al., 1991). The occurrence of isolated and dispersed clasts is suggestive of open marine environments where floating icebergs melt.
