1. Derive a formula that can be used to convert a temperature reading from kelvin to rankine scales​


To convert from kelvin to Rankine, we need to use the following formula:

Rankine = Kelvin * (9/5)

This formula is derived from the fact that the kelvin and Rankine scales are both absolute temperature scales, which means that they both have the same zero point (absolute zero). The only difference between the two scales is the size of their units: the kelvin has a unit size of 1/273.16 of the size of the degree Celsius, while the Rankine has a unit size of 1/180 of the size of the degree Fahrenheit.

Therefore, to convert a temperature from kelvin to Rankine, we simply need to multiply the temperature in kelvin by the ratio of the size of the degree Fahrenheit to the size of the degree Celsius, which is (9/5). This gives us the temperature in Rankine.

For example, to convert 273.15 kelvin to Rankine, we would use the following calculation:

Rankine = 273.15 K * (9/5) = 491.67 R

This means that 273.15 kelvin is equivalent to 491.67 Rankine.

The Kelvin and Rankine scales of temperature were both named after the Scottish physicist and engineer William Thomson, 1st Baron Kelvin. Thomson developed the concept of absolute zero and the first and second laws of thermodynamics, which led to the creation of the Kelvin and Rankine scales. The formula for converting between these two scales is:

T(K) = T(R) * 5/9

where T(K) is the temperature in Kelvin and T(R) is the temperature in Rankine.

However, unlike the Kelvin scale, the Rankine degree is defined as equal to one degree Fahrenheit, rather than the one degree Celsius used by the Kelvin scale. A temperature of −459.67 °F is exactly equal to 0 °R. The Rankine scale is used for thermodynamic calculations.


The Kelvin temperature scale is used in the International System of Units (SI), to measure the temperature. Kelvin is one of the seven base units in the system.
