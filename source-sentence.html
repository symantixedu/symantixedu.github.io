13. give 5 examples of a primary source and a secondary source and use them in a sentence.​


Answer:

Primary sources:

1. Diary entries - "In her diary entries, Anne Frank describes her life in hiding during the Holocaust."

2. Photographs - "The iconic photograph of a sailor kissing a nurse in Times Square is a primary source that captures the jubilant mood of the end of World War II."

3. Government documents - "The U.S. Constitution is a primary source that outlines the framework for the federal government."

4. Letters - "The letters between John and Abigail Adams provide insight into the political and personal lives of two prominent figures during the American Revolution."

5. Artifacts - "The Rosetta Stone, a decree issued by King Ptolemy V in 196 BC, is a primary source that provided the key to understanding ancient Egyptian hieroglyphics."

Secondary sources:

1. History textbooks - "The history textbook provided an overview of the causes and effects of the Great Depression."

2. Biographies - "The biography of Martin Luther King Jr. provided insight into the life and work of the civil rights leader."

3. Literary criticism - "The literary criticism analyzed the themes and symbols in F. Scott Fitzgerald's The Great Gatsby."

4. Newspaper articles - "The newspaper article provided a summary of the key events of the Vietnam War."

5. Academic journals - "The academic journal article analyzed the impact of climate change on global food systems."

17. what are the differences between primary sources and secondary sources? answer in 3-5 sentences​


Difference between Primary sources and Secondary sources.

Primary sources were either created during the period being studied or were created at a later date by a participant who was present when events were being studied. Primary sources enable the researcher to get closer to what actually happened during a historical event or period. A secondary source may be a work that interprets or analyzes an historical event or phenomenon. It's generally a derivation of the event and is commonly supported by primary sources.

The key to determining whether an item could also be considered to be a primary source is to ask how soon after the event was the information obtained recorded.

When evaluating primary or secondary sources, the subsequent questions can be asked to verify the character and value of material being considered:

How does the author know these details (names, dates, times)? Was the author present at the event or soon on the scene?Where does this information come from—personal experience, eyewitness accounts, or reports written by others? Are the author's conclusions supported by one piece of evidence, or have many sources been taken under consideration (e.g., diary entries, together with third-party eyewitness accounts, impressions of contemporaries, newspaper accounts)?
