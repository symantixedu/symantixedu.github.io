3. characteristic of gangsa palookgongs/wooden mallet​


Answer:

A gangsa is a type of metallophone which is used mainly in Balinese and Javanese Gamelan music in Indonesia. In Balinese gong kebyar styles, there are two types of gangsa typically used: the smaller, higher-pitched kantilan and the larger pemade. Each instrument consists of several tuned metal bars (either iron or bronze) each placed over an individual resonator. The bars are hit with a wooden panggul, each producing a different pitch.

19. 11. How is Gangsa Pallok played?A. Gongs are beaten with bare handsB. Gongs are played by striking each otherC. Gongs are struck with wooden malletsD. Gongs are scratch using a metal.​


C. Gongs are struck with wooden mallets

THE GANGSA

Gangsa is an instrumental group. Gangsa is a narrow-rimmed, hand-held gong. Regional and cultural preferences determine its pitch. Gangsa is played standing with five to six flat gongs. This depends on the instruments available and ethnic traditions.

1. Gangsa Toppaya - gongs are struck with the palm while resting on the lap. They're played during weddings, peace pacts, and parties.

2. Gangsa Palook/pattung — Gongs are pounded with left-handed wooden mallets. Musicians (typically men) stand or perform step dance routines with ladies dancers.

10% of Filipinos fought Spanish and American authority. Those in the Cordillera Mountains in Northern Luzon adhere to various culture and musical tradition (northern tradition) in continental South East Asia, while those in Mindoro, Palawan, Mindanao, and the Sulu group of islands in the extreme south relate their musical tradition (southern tradition) to the immediate island of insular South East Asia.

These musical traditions are tied to the people's social, political, and economic lives, as well as their spiritual beliefs and natural environment. Cordillera music has two distinct sounds based on the instruments' materials. It's like verbal music. Ceremonies, rites, and festivities feature music.

Explanation:

The ranat ek (Thai: ระนาดเอก, pronounced [ranâːt ʔèːk], "also xylophone") is a Thai musical instrument in the percussion family that consists of 21 wooden bars suspended by cords over a boat-shaped trough resonator and struck by two mallets. It is used as a leading instrument in the piphat ensemble.
