7. what do you notice about the shape of pebbles crater and the shape of impact crater ?


What do you notice about the shape of pebbles crater and the shape of impact crater ?

The shape of the impact crater is very similar to the shape of the crater of the pebbles, both slightly oval and irregular in shape. When the crater is relatively small, the formation of the crater ends when the drilling ceases. The resulting type of land is called a simple crater. The smallest craters take no more than a few seconds to shape completely, whereas craters that are tens of kilometers wide are likely to form in a few minutes. However, as meteorite craters get bigger, the mechanism of creation does not end with the excavation. For such craters, the parabolic hole is evidently too wide to support itself, and collapses in a mechanism that creates a number of features. This period of collapse is called the stage of alteration, and the final depression is known as a complex crater. The alteration stage of complex crater formation is not well known because the mechanism is largely beyond the existing technical capacity to model or replicate, and because explosion craters on Earth are too small to create genuinely complex crater landforms. While conceptually the stage of alteration is assumed to occur after excavation, it may be that collapse occurs before the excavation is finished. The current state of understanding of dynamic crater formation depends largely on inferences obtained from field studies of Earth's impact systems and spacecraft imagery of impact craters on other solid bodies in the solar system. Impact craters are formed when a meteorite collides with the surface of a moon or earth (or other body in space). In this practice, the marbles and pebbles that fall into the sand reflect meteorites that smash into the surface and create various types of impact craters. Craters are located in the solar system. They are most popular in worlds that do not have a dense atmosphere, such as the Moon. A thick atmosphere, like Earth's, potentially stops any asteroids, comets, and meteors from touching the surface. The friction between the target and the dense air allows the object to burn up or to become smaller as it moves through the atmosphere. Scientists use instruments to locate and analyze craters and understand more about geological processes on planets, stars, asteroids and other universes. Observations and crater photos give a snapshot of the geological layers of the earth. Even operating remotely, scientists can learn more about the planetary environment, what it is made of, how it is created, and the forces that create it. NASA's Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter has mapped the Moon for several years, paying special attention to the cratered floor. A special ultraviolet instrument helped to discover new knowledge about the Moon, including frost in some of its craters.
